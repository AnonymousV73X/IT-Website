System Analysis and Design

  • Improving system development
    Approaches to improving system development

    Prototyping:
    Prototyping is a technique where a smaller, working version of the desired system is built first.
    This can be done using various computer languages or development tools, but there are specific prototyping tools available to make the process easier. The prototype can be created using 4G, query and screen design tools of a database management system (DBMS), or Computer-Aided Software Engineering (CASE) tools.

    There are three main activities involved in prototyping:

    • Mock-up:
    This is a non-functional demonstration of the proposed system's appearance. It helps users visualize the final system's look and feel but lacks actual processing or storage capabilities.
    • Trial Prototyping:
    A simplified working model of the system is created with some of its capabilities. It can be used to design the user interface for the new system.
    • Rapid Prototyping:
    This approach aims for quick application development by delaying the detailed system design until user requirements are clear. It prioritizes gaining user acceptance and building core capabilities rapidly over optimizing computer efficiency.


    Joint Application Design (JAD):
    JAD is a structured process where users, managers, and analysts collaborate in intensive meetings over several days to specify or review system requirements.
    It was developed in the late 1970s by IBM. However, JAD methodologies might overlook important software engineering principles, leading to inconsistencies between system modules and non-compliant components, making maintenance costly and challenging.


    The People in a System Development Process:

    There are two key stakeholders involved in the system development process:

    • Management:
    They are responsible for ensuring that the end results of the development process meet the company's needs, and they oversee the project to ensure it stays on schedule and within the budget.

    • Users:
    Users are the individuals for whom the systems are developed. They are the ones who will be using the system on a day-to-day basis. The system should increase their efficiency and productivity. Users' concerns include ease of use and system reliability. They can contribute valuable ideas and insights into the business procedures involved in the development process.
  • Systems Analyst
    Roles of a Systems Analyst:

    Helping the User Determine Information Needs:
    The analyst works with users to understand their information requirements and needs for the system.

    Systems Analysis:
    Gathering information about the existing systems and analyzing them to assess their effectiveness.

    Systems Design:
    Designing new systems or suggesting changes to existing ones and helping in the implementation of those changes.

    Systems Specification:
    Providing detailed specifications of the system, including inputs, files, processing, output, hardware, cost, accuracy, and response times.

    System Testing and Maintenance:
    Testing the system to ensure it works correctly and providing ongoing maintenance and support.


    Skills of a Systems Analyst:

    a) Analytical Skills:
    - Understanding the organization and its functions.
    - Identifying and defining problems that need solving.
    - Analyzing and finding solutions to the identified problems through four phases: Intelligence, Design, Choice, and Implementation.

    b) Technical Skills:
    - Being familiar with various technologies used in systems development, such as computers, programming languages, operating systems, databases, networking, web development tools, and more.
    - Staying up-to-date with the latest technology trends through reading, joining professional societies, attending courses, and conferences.

    c) Management Skills:
    - Resource Management:
    Effectively utilizing resources like documentation, budget, and people within the project team.
    - Project Management:
    Ensuring projects stay on track and are completed within the given time and budget.
    - Risk Management:
    Anticipating potential risks and finding ways to minimize their impact on the project.
    - Change Management:
    Helping people transition smoothly from old systems to new ones.

    d) Interpersonal Skills:
    - Working effectively with people from different backgrounds and roles within the organization.

    e) Communication Skills:
    - Interviewing, Listening, and Questionnaires:
    Gathering information from users through effective communication.
    - Written and Oral Presentations:
    Clearly conveying project progress, schedules, and details to stakeholders through various communication methods.
  • High-Performance Team
    Characteristics of a High-Performance Team:

    Shared, Elevated Vision or Goal:
    A high-performance team is driven by a clear and inspiring vision or goal. Every team member understands the project's objectives, and this clarity keeps their priorities aligned. The vision should challenge the team, motivating them to work towards a common purpose.

    Sense of Team Identity:
    As team members work closely together, they develop a shared team identity. This creates a strong bond among team members, leading to a common language and shared humor. A strong team identity fosters a sense of belonging and mutual support.

    Result-Driven Structure:
    An effective team has a well-defined structure that promotes results. This includes clear roles and responsibilities for each team member, an efficient communication system, performance monitoring mechanisms, and decision-making based on facts rather than emotions. This structured approach ensures that the team remains focused on achieving its goals.

    Competent Team Members:
    High-performance teams consist of skilled and capable individuals who bring their expertise to the table. Each team member's competence contributes to the overall success of the project.

    Commitment to the Team:
    Team members are committed to the success of the team and the project. They are dedicated to working together and supporting one another to achieve the shared goals.

    Mutual Trust Among the Team:
    Trust is a fundamental element of high-performance teams. Team members trust one another's abilities, intentions, and contributions. This trust fosters collaboration and openness within the team.

    Interdependence Among Team Members:
    High-performance teams recognize the importance of interdependence. Each member's work complements and supports the work of others, leading to a cohesive and efficient team effort.

    Effective Communication Means:
    Open and effective communication is crucial for a successful team. Team members must be able to express their ideas, concerns, and progress transparently to maintain alignment and resolve any issues promptly.

    Sense of Autonomy:
    Each team member should have the freedom and autonomy to make decisions they believe are best for the team and the project. This empowers individuals and encourages ownership of their work.

    Sense of Empowerment:
    High-performance teams empower each member, allowing them to take initiative and contribute meaningfully to the project's success.

    Small Team Size:
    An optimal team size, usually no larger than 8-10 people, enables better communication, coordination, and collaboration among team members.

    High Level of Enjoyment:
    Finally, a high-performance team enjoys working together. A positive and enjoyable work environment enhances team spirit and motivates team members to give their best effort.
  • CASE Tools
    Computer-Aided Software Engineering (CASE) Tools:
    CASE tools are automated software tools used by systems analysts to support the development of information systems. These tools offer numerous advantages in the systems development process:

    Objectives of CASE in an organization:
    - Improve the quality of system development.
    - Increase the speed of designing and developing systems.
    - Simplify and enhance the testing process through automated checking.
    - Improve integration across development activities using common methodologies.
    - Enhance the quality and completeness of documentation.
    - Standardize the development process.
    - Improve project management.
    - Simplify program maintenance.
    - Promote reusability of modules and documentation.
    - Enhance software portability across environments.
    - Shorten development time.

    However, organizations may sometimes reject CASE due to several reasons:
    - High cost of purchasing CASE tools.
    - High cost of training personnel to use CASE effectively.
    - Lengthy planning, analysis, and design duration, which may not fully show CASE benefits until later stages of development.
    - Some CASE tools may lack seamless information sharing capabilities between different tools.
    - Difficulty in providing a complete set of tools for all aspects of the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC).
    - Lack of methodology standards within the organization, making it challenging to align CASE with the existing practices.
    - Fear of CASE tools threatening job security.
    - Lack of confidence in the effectiveness and reliability of CASE products.

    Components of CASE include:
    - Diagramming Tools: Used to represent system processes, data, and control structures visually, such as Data Flow Diagrams (DFD) and Entity-Relationship Diagrams (ER).
    - CASE Form and Report Generation Tools: Support the creation of computer display forms and reports to prototype the system's user interface.
    - CASE Analysis Tools: Help in automatic checking for incomplete, inconsistent, or incorrect specifications in diagrams, forms, and reports.
    - CASE Repository: A centralized database containing all system-related information, facilitating seamless data and tool integration across SDLC activities.
    - CASE Documentation: Modules that create technical and user documentation in standard formats based on the repository's contents.
    - CASE Code Generation Tools: Automatically produce high-level program source code from diagrams and forms.

    CASE has significant impacts on individuals within the organization, affecting their roles and responsibilities. It automates routine tasks for analysts and programmers, involves users more actively in the development process, and encourages top managers to be engaged in planning and user-oriented development methods.

    Additionally, "reverse engineering" and "reengineering" tools are categories of CASE products that breathe new life into existing systems by creating design specifications from program code and data definitions, allowing modifications and improvements. Visual and emerging development tools, such as Object-Oriented (OO) and Visual Development Tools, are also gaining popularity in modern software development.
