System Analysis and Design

  • Project Initiation & Planning
    Project Initiation and Planning (PIP) Phase - Software Development Project:

    Project Initiation Phase:

    - Establishing the project initiation team:
    The company selects a team of experienced software developers, designers, and testers to work on the project, ensuring relevant skills and expertise in mobile app development.

    - Establishing a relationship with the customer:
    An analyst meets with the client to understand their business needs, goals, and requirements, establishing clear communication channels and work standards for effective collaboration.

    - Establishing the project initiation plan:
    The company creates a plan outlining the initial steps, goals, and objectives of the project, identifying key stakeholders and defining their roles and responsibilities.

    - Establishing management procedures: Project management procedures are set up to handle tasks like progress tracking, issue resolution, and reporting, ensuring the project stays on track.

    Project Planning Phase:
    - Describing the project scope, alternatives, and feasibility:
    The team defines the mobile app's scope, considers development approaches, and assesses feasibility for each option.

    - Dividing the project into manageable tasks:
    The development process is broken down into smaller tasks, such as designing the user interface, developing backend functionalities, and testing the app.

    - Estimating resources and creating a resource plan:
    The team estimates the required number of developers, designers, and testers for each task and creates a resource plan.

    - Identifying and assessing risks:
    Potential risks, like technical challenges or changes in requirements, are identified, and strategies to mitigate them are developed.

    - Developing a preliminary schedule and budget:
    Based on tasks and resource estimates, a preliminary project schedule and budget are created, outlining the timeline and cost for each development phase.

    - Developing a communication plan:
    A communication plan is established to update stakeholders regularly on the project's progress and any challenges encountered.

    - Creating a Statement of Work (SoW):
    A detailed SoW is prepared, outlining deliverables, timeline, costs, and other contractual terms.

    - Setting a Baseline Project Plan (BPP):
    The team finalizes the project plan and obtains approval from the client, stakeholders, and management to proceed with development.
  • The SoW & BPP
    Baseline Project Plan (BPP):
    The BPP is a comprehensive document containing all information collected and analyzed during project initiation and planning.
    It serves as the foundation for subsequent Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) activities, reflecting the best estimate of project scope, benefits, costs, risks, resources required, and schedules.

    Contents of the Baseline Project Plan:
    a) Introduction: Provides an executive summary of the project, outlining scope, feasibility, justification, resource requirements, and schedules.
    b) System Description: Describes the selected system configuration, including considered alternatives.
    c) Feasibility Assessment: Discusses the feasibility study, analyzing technical, economic, legal, operational, and scheduling aspects.
    d) Management Issues: Covers team configuration, management, communication plans, and project standards and procedures.

    The BPP is reviewed by the user, management, and development groups to ensure alignment with organizational standards and gain approval before proceeding to the next SDLC phase. A structured walkthrough, involving specific roles, is often used for this review.

    Statement of Work (SoW):
    The SoW is a concise document prepared for the customer, describing project deliverables and outlining all work required to complete it. It ensures a common understanding between the project team and the customer.

    Contents of the Statement of Work:
    - Title/Name of the organization and Date of the SoW
    - Project Name
    - Project Manager
    - Customer
    - Project Sponsor
    - Project Start/End (Projected )

    The SoW provides a project description, objectives, phases of work, and other essential details about deliverables and benefits. Both the BPP and SoW are crucial documents setting the project's direction, helping stakeholders make informed decisions, ensuring clarity in project goals, and serving as references for all subsequent SDLC activities.
  • Feasibility Study
    Economic Feasibility:
    The economic feasibility analysis involves evaluating the financial benefits and costs associated with the project.
    This includes identifying both tangible and intangible benefits and costs.
    Tangible benefits: Measurable and certain benefits, such as cost savings, error reduction, increased efficiency, and improved management planning and control.
    Intangible benefits: Non-measurable benefits with direct organizational or societal implications, like improved employee morale or reduced waste creation.
    Project costs are also assessed, including tangible costs like hardware and software purchases, labor costs, training expenses, and intangible costs like loss of customer goodwill or operational inefficiencies. Both one-time and recurring costs are considered.

    Technical Feasibility:
    Technical feasibility evaluates the organization's ability to construct the proposed system. Key factors include:
    - Project size: Larger projects are riskier due to complexity and coordination challenges.
    - Project structure: Renovating existing systems or making procedural changes may be less risky than completely new projects.
    - Development Group: Familiarity with target hardware, software, and prior experience with similar projects influence technical risk.
    - User Groups: Familiarity with IS development processes and previous experience with similar systems impact project risk.
    Managing technical risks involves understanding potential sources of risks and devising strategies to mitigate them.

    Operational Feasibility:
    Operational feasibility assesses whether the proposed project aligns with the organization's deadlines and constraints for implementing change. It ensures that the project activities can be completed within the available time frame and resources.

    Legal and Contractual Feasibility:
    This feasibility focuses on evaluating potential legal and contractual implications due to the construction of the system. Considerations include copyright infringements, labor laws, antitrust legislation, foreign trade regulations, and contractual obligations, especially when using third-party services.

    Political Feasibility:
    Political feasibility refers to the evaluation of how key stakeholders within the organization view the proposed system. It addresses whether these stakeholders support, oppose, or might attempt to disrupt the project. Understanding political feasibility helps anticipate and address potential resistance or challenges from influential parties.
  • Design Phase of SDLC
    Imagine a company called "XYZ Tech Solutions" that offers various IT services. They want to create a new website to showcase their services and attract potential clients.

    Here's how the design phase would unfold:

    System Analysis:
    During the analysis phase, "XYZ Tech Solutions" identifies website requirements and gathers input from stakeholders. They define the purpose, target audience, key features, and content. The system analyst documents these in a System Requirements Specification (SRS) document.

    Transition to Design Phase:
    After reviewing the SRS document, management decides to develop the website in-house, marking the transition to the design phase.

    Logical Design:
    In the logical design phase, the system analyst defines website functions and features without specific implementation details. They determine pages, content, and navigation. For example, homepage, services, contact, and blog sections.

    Physical Design:
    The physical design phase plans website implementation based on the logical design. It covers data entry methods, server file organization, page layout, and output format. For example, HTML, CSS, JavaScript for front-end, and a database for client info.

    Relationship between Analysis and Design:
    Logical and physical design are related. Logical design comes first and serves as the foundation for physical design. Deviations from the analysis are minimized, focusing on moving forward with the established logical design.

    Continuous Review:
    Throughout the design phase, the system analyst ensures the physical design aligns with the SRS document. Any deviations are documented and justified.
  • Implementation Phase of SDLC
    System Implementation:
    System Implementation is the phase of the SDLC where the physical specifications and design are turned into a working computer system.
    It involves coding, testing, installing the system, documenting it, training users, and preparing the necessary support systems to assist users.

    1. Coding:
    Example: Imagine a company developing a mobile application for food delivery services. During the coding phase, the software developers will write the necessary code to build the app based on the physical design specifications. They will implement features like user registration, order placement, payment processing, and delivery tracking by translating the logical design into actual code.

    2. Testing:
    Example: Continuing with the food delivery app, during the testing phase, the developers will systematically test the app's various components to detect and fix errors. They will conduct unit testing to ensure that individual modules, such as order processing or payment handling, perform their functions correctly. Integration testing will be carried out to verify that all modules work together seamlessly. System testing will evaluate the entire app's functionality, including how it interacts with external systems like GPS for tracking deliveries. They will document the testing process and any issues encountered for further analysis and resolution.

    3. Installation:
    Example: Once the food delivery app has passed the testing phase, it is ready for installation on the servers or app stores. The development team will deploy the application to the designated servers, making it accessible to users for download and use.

    4. Documentation:
    Example: Comprehensive documentation is crucial for the food delivery app's success. The development team will prepare user manuals, technical guides, and system documentation. The user manuals will help customers understand how to use the app, while technical guides will assist support staff in troubleshooting and maintaining the system. The system documentation will provide insights into the architecture, database structures, and other technical details.

    5. Training:
    Example: To ensure smooth adoption of the food delivery app, the development team may conduct training sessions for the restaurant owners, delivery personnel, and customer support staff. These training sessions will familiarize users with the app's features and functionalities, making them more confident in using it effectively.

    6. Support:
    Example: After the app is launched, a support system will be set up to assist users in case they encounter any issues or have questions. This support system might include a help desk, online chat support, or an email support team that responds to customer inquiries promptly.
  • System Installation
    Approaches to System Installation:
    1) Direct Installation:
    Example: A retail chain decides to replace its outdated point-of-sale (POS) system with a modern, cloud-based POS solution. They choose direct installation because the new system has a completely different architecture and offers more advanced features. The company is confident in the new system's reliability and accuracy after extensive testing and vendor recommendations. On the go-live day, they switch off the old POS system and switch on the new one, resulting in a seamless transition for both customers and employees.

    2) Parallel Installation:
    Example: A medium-sized manufacturing company is implementing a new Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system to streamline their operations. To ensure a smooth transition and minimize risks, they decide to run the new ERP system in parallel with the existing legacy system for a few months. During this period, they compare outputs from both systems to verify the new system's performance. Once the management is confident in the new system's reliability, they turn off the legacy system and fully adopt the new ERP solution.

    3) Single Location Installation/Pilot Installation:
    Example: An international airline company wants to upgrade its reservation and booking system. Instead of rolling out the new system across all locations simultaneously, they decide to pilot the installation at one of their smaller regional offices. The new system is tested thoroughly in this single location, and feedback is collected from users. Based on the results and feedback, any necessary adjustments are made before implementing the system in other locations.

    4) Phased Installation/Staged Installation:
    Example: A large educational institution is replacing its outdated student information system with a modern, integrated student management solution. They opt for a phased installation approach, starting with the registration and admission module. Once the new module is successfully implemented and stable, they proceed to install the grading and attendance module. This incremental approach allows the institution to manage potential risks and ensure smooth integration between the new and old systems.

    Planning Installation:
    In the process of system installation, it's essential to plan and manage several aspects, including data conversion, hardware setup, training, and scheduling. For example, when implementing a new Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system for a retail company, they need to convert their existing customer data into the format compatible with the new CRM. They may also need to reformat the data to match the new database structure.
  • Approaches to Installation
    Advantages of Different Approaches to Installation:
    Direct Installation:
    Quick and straightforward process.
    Immediate transition to the new system, minimizing downtime.
    Suitable for cases where the new system is vastly different from the old one.

    Parallel Installation:
    Allows for side-by-side comparison of the old and new systems.
    Ensures a fallback option if issues arise in the new system.
    Lower risk as the old system continues to work during the transition.

    Single Location Installation/Pilot Installation:
    Enables gradual implementation, reducing risks across the organization.
    Easier to manage and control the transition in a smaller test environment.
    Provides valuable feedback and insights before full deployment.

    Phased Installation/Staged Installation:
    Incremental approach reduces the impact of change on users.
    Early modules can be tested and refined before proceeding further.
    Allows for a smoother transition across the organization.

    Disadvantages of Different Approaches to Installation:
    Direct Installation:
    High risk if the new system fails to meet expectations.
    Limited opportunity to revert to the old system if critical issues arise.
    Employees may face difficulties adjusting to the sudden change.

    Parallel Installation:
    Resource-intensive, requiring both old and new systems to run simultaneously.
    Potential confusion for users managing two systems simultaneously.
    Extended timeframe for full transition, impacting overall efficiency.

    Single Location Installation/Pilot Installation:
    Limited scope may not fully represent challenges across the organization.
    May not uncover certain issues that arise during a broader rollout.
    Can result in delays if adjustments are needed after the pilot phase.

    Phased Installation/Staged Installation:
    Complexity increases as more components are integrated.
    Potential compatibility issues between phased components.
    Lengthy implementation timeline due to gradual rollout.
  • Types of Documentation
    Basic Types of Documentation: System Documentation and User Documentation
    1. System Documentation:
    a) Internal Documentation: This type of documentation is usually embedded within the program source code or automatically generated during the compilation process. It provides insights into how the code works and helps developers understand the internal logic and structure of the software. Examples of internal documentation include comments within the code that explain specific functions, algorithms, or complex logic.

    Example: A software development team is building a web application for an e-commerce platform. In the source code, they include comments that explain the steps involved in processing a customer's order, calculating the shipping cost, and handling various exceptions during the checkout process. These comments serve as internal documentation to guide other developers who might work on the code in the future.

    b) External Documentation: This type of documentation encompasses a broader view of the system and is created using structured diagramming techniques like data flow diagrams (DFD) and entity-relationship diagrams (ERD). It captures the system's design specifications, interactions between various components, and the flow of data within the system.

    Example: For the same e-commerce web application, the development team creates external documentation in the form of DFDs that illustrate how data flows from user input on the website to the database and back to the user as a response. Additionally, they create ERDs to depict the relationships between entities such as customers, products, and orders in the database.

    2. User Documentation: User documentation provides information to end-users, explaining how to use the software effectively. It includes written or visual instructions that guide users on how to navigate the application's interface, perform specific tasks, and access various features.

    Example: In the case of the e-commerce web application, user documentation would include on-screen tooltips, pop-up help messages, and step-by-step guides for processes like creating an account, adding items to the shopping cart, and completing the checkout process. This documentation helps users understand the application's functionalities and empowers them to use it efficiently.

    In modern software development, user documentation is often delivered online in hypertext format. This allows users to access help and instructions directly within the application, making it easier to find relevant information without the need for bulky printed manuals.
  • Approaches to Installation
    Computer Training Methods:
    Tutorial - One person taught at a time:
    Example: The retail company hires a trainer to provide one-on-one tutorials to its store managers and cashiers. During these sessions, the trainer demonstrates how to use the new POS system, including how to process transactions, handle returns, and manage inventory. Each staff member receives personalized attention, allowing them to ask questions and practice using the system hands-on.
    Course - Several people taught at a time:
    Example: To train a larger group of store employees simultaneously, the retail company organizes group training courses. They schedule sessions in which a trainer conducts classroom-style training for store employees from multiple locations. This approach allows for more efficient training of a larger number of users at once.
    Computer Aided Instructions:
    Example: The retail company provides computer-aided instruction through interactive e-learning modules. Employees can access these modules on the company's intranet or through an online learning platform. The modules include interactive quizzes and simulations that help employees learn how to use the POS system step by step at their own pace.
    User Support:
    Electronic Performance Support Systems (EPSS):
    Example: The new POS system comes with an embedded EPSS that provides on-screen help and guidance to users. When an employee encounters a new feature or has a question, they can access the EPSS, which offers contextual help, tutorials, and links to reference materials directly within the POS software. This feature assists users in real-time without requiring them to leave the application.
    Online Support Forums:
    Example: The retail company sets up an online support forum where employees can post questions, share experiences, and seek assistance related to the new POS system. Trainers and experienced users, as well as representatives from the POS vendor, actively participate in the forum to provide timely answers and solutions.
    On-Demand Fax and Voice Response Systems:
    Example: To provide quick and efficient support, the retail company establishes an on-demand support system that allows employees to request help via fax or interactive voice response (IVR). The system can provide automated responses to common inquiries or route more complex issues to appropriate support personnel for resolution.
